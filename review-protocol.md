## REVIEW TITLE AND BASIC DETAILS

### Review title
Effectiveness of Artificial Intelligence supported tools in the treatment of mental-ill health in youth: A Living Systematic Review

### Condition or domain being studied
Mental ill-health, mental health disorders, Anxiety, Depression, Bipolar, Eating Disorders, Psychosis, Substance Use, Suicide & Self-Harm

### Review objectives
The primary objective is to examine the effectiveness of artificial intelligence (AI)-supported tools used in the treatment of mental health conditions in young people. 
Secondary objective will examine the safety of these tools. In addition, factors that may influence effectiveness and/or safety will potentially be examined, such as population (age, mental health indication etc.), intervention (type of AI tool etc.), comparator, and/or study design characteristics.

### Keywords
Artificial intelligence; Mental health; Intervention; Children; Adolescents;

### Country (of review)
Australia

## ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA
### Population
**Included**

Age: Youth aged 12-25 years. Trials commonly operationalise age=18 to differentiate adolescents from adults for inclusion. However, contemporary definitions of adolescence and youth typically cross this boundary into the first few years of early adulthood (e.g., World Health Organisation). Youth-specific mental health service models are becoming common in many countries, catering to youth up to 25 years (e.g., headspace). Therefore, we will explicitly include trials where the mean/median age is >/= 12 and < 26 years, rather than requiring absolute study age range be 12-25 for inclusion. Where age is not reported, we will accept youth descriptors for inclusion (e.g., adolescents, young people, young adult, college students etc.).

Indication: 
Given this review is treatment focused, trials must recruit youth who either:
1.	score above a minimum threshold on symptom measure (e.g., BDI > 13); or 
2.	have a mental health diagnosis by self-report, clinician interview or other (SCID, symptom checklist etc.), or
3.	are treatment seeking.
Trial-author definitions for symptom thresholds and diagnostic status will be accepted.

**Excluded:**

Age: children < 12 years of age, adults >/= 26 years of age.

Indication: Healthy populations (e.g., recruited without mental health symptoms), populations with conditions such as neurodevelopmental disorders (e.g., autism), intellectual disability, physical health or other medical conditions (e.g., diabetes, cancer) will be excluded.

### Intervention(s) or exposure(s)
**Included**

Mental health interventions that use AI-enabled tools with a young person. Any digital tool where AI is a central feature of its design and implementation. For this review AI is defined broadly as a system designed to perform tasks associated with human intelligence such as reasoning, learning, inference or adaptation by using sub-symbolic computational approaches (machine learning). 

Sub-symbolic AI: A system that functions by identifying patterns in data through machine learning methods. Sub-symbolic systems generate probabilistic inferences based on weighted variables derived during training, unlike ‘symbolic’ or rule-based programmed systems, which operate on human authored logic. 

Rule-based programmed system: A deterministic system relying on fixed condition-action rules (if-then) without inference or learning. Many static mental health digital applications fall into this category, as well as simple scripted chat-bots. For the purpose of this review, these do not constitute the application of sub-symbolic AI, and will be excluded unless operating within a hybrid system.

Hybrid AI systems: combining sub-symbolic computational methods with rule-based programmed control logic. Mental health chatbots often fall into this category where machine learning methods classify user messages (e.g., NLP models identify user intent) and rule-based programming is used to respond with pre-defined scripts (e.g., Woebot, Wysa)

To be eligible, AI techniques in these systems must be implemented to interpret/classify user input, or adapt, generate or select output/content. The overall AI-supported tool must be an active part of treatment e.g., influencing ongoing treatment process. The tool must be young person facing, or collect and use young person data during the treatment phase.

Tools can be mental health specific (e.g., a mental health chat bot) or general purpose and not specifically designed for mental health use cases (e.g., ChatGPT)

Three use-cases are included in the current review: 1. chatbots or conversation agents, 2. treatment recommender systems, and 3. adaptive interventions.

**Excluded:**

Rule-based programmed systems without inference or learning mechanisms are excluded. These describe most static mental health apps and simple chatbots with scripted inputs and outputs.

Use of machine learning techniques for data-analysis only

Stand-alone prognostic/prediction models that do not inform treatment selection.

Scribes, journaling tools or other clinican-facing support tools.

### Comparator(s) or control(s)
**Included**

No-treatment (NT), Wait-list (WL), Treatment as usual (TAU, where this occurs outside of the trial and is not formalised)

Attention placebo (i.e., non-therapeutic controls matched to intervention on time, contact, dosage etc.)

Other mental health treatments (e.g., psychological therapy, complementary & alternative therapies, medication). We include TAU under this comparator if it occurs within the trial context and is formalised (e.g., comparison arm gets manualised CBT and medication as TAU).

### Study design
**Included**

Only controlled trials will be included. Both randomised (RCTs) and non-randomised (CCTs) controlled trials will be eligible providing the trial compares at least two groups with allocation occurring during the same time period. Parallel, cluster and cross-over trials will be eligible.

**Excluded:**

Trials with historical or retrospective control/comparators, single group trials (pre-post, observational cohort studies), trials without an intervention comparison group.

### Context
Co-interventions: Given AI-tools may be used alongside other treatments, we will include separate comparisons which will include 1. AI-tools + other treatments/treatment components versus comparator/control, and 2. AI-tools alone vs comparator/control. Synthesis will be handled separately for these groupings. Where two different AI-supported tools are compared directly (in the absence of a control group), these will form a third separate grouping for synthesis.

## SEARCHING AND SCREENING

### Search for unpublished studies

Only published studies will be sought. We will accept published conference abstracts, where sufficient information is available to determine eligibility. Dissertations, pre-prints and trial protocols/registrations will not be sought and are not eligible.

### Main bibliographic databases that will be searched

The following databases will be searched: 
-	Orygen’s Youth Mental Health Evidence Map (a database of youth mental health intervention controlled trials, via https://orygen.org.au/Training/Evidence-Finder), 
-	Cochrane Controlled Trials Register (CENTRAL via https://www.cochranelibrary.com/central), 
-	Association for Computing Machinery Digital Library (ACM via https://dl.acm.org/search/advanced), 
-	MEDLINE, Embase and PsycInfo via the OVID platform
  
These six databases give coverage to each domain of the research question.

### Search language restrictions

English only publications will be eligible, due to resource constraints. While non-english publications will not be sought, where they are identified, freely available translation tools may be considered.

### Other methods of identifying studies

Other studies will be identified by: contacting authors or experts; reference list checking (backward citation searching); searching conference proceedings.

Experts will be contacted for potential studies. The reference lists of systematic reviews with inclusion criteria closely matched to this review, and published in the last 5 years, will be checked and their included studies added to screening procedure. These reviews will be identified by PubMed/google scholar searches with terms youth, artificial intelligence or chatbot, mental health, and systematic review. 
Database searches cover both mental health and AI conference proceedings typically indexed in MEDLINE, Embase and ACM databases. 

### Selection process

Studies screened independently by at least 2 people (or person/machine combination) with a process to resolve differences.

### Other relevant information about searching and screening

Screening of database retrievals will be done in Covidence.

Title/abstract: all records will be screened independently by two reviewers.

Full text: The full-texts of all records included from title/abstract screen will be sought and double screened independently by two reviewers. Reasons for exclusion will be recorded.
Discrepancies at either stage will be resolved through discussion, with a third reviewer or an expert from the review’s advisory committee. 

Updating searches: Nominally the frequency of updates will be monthly, with the schedule reviewed and adjusted based on retrieval rates and number of new trials identified for synthesis incorporation.
For search update retrievals, a screening procedure will be piloted using ASReview (machine assisted screening), where one reviewer manually screens all records and a second reviewer uses ASReview to screen a portion of all records. Data-driven stopping rules determined from the pilot phase will be used (e.g., 35% of total screening, or 50 consecutive irrelevant records screened). If successfully identifying 100% of records, ASReview assisted screening will be used to screen all update retrievals at title/abstract stage. Full-text screening of update searches will be done independently in duplicate by two reviewers.

Data Extraction: data will be extracted either: 1. Independently, in duplicated, by two reviewers, or 2. By one reviewer and by machine-assisted extraction tool. Discrepancies will be resolved through discussion, with a third reviewer or an expert from the review’s advisory committee.
Extracted domains will include: 1. study identification: author, publication year, location/country, AI-tool name, protocol/registration number, trial N; 2. participant characteristics: age, gender, and mental health status for study entry; 3. Trial characteristics: study design, intervention & control/comparator arms, number of participants per group, recruitment setting, trial duration, dosage (frequency, session length etc.); 4. intervention characteristics: intervention description, AI-tool modality (conversational agent, recommender system, adaptive intervention), AI category (sub-symbolic, symbolic, hybrid), AI-tool features (e.g., response generation – retrieval vs generative, learning/inference present, ML techniques applied, input/output side, interaction modality etc.), AI-tool delivery (device, platform, human support), use case (general, mental health specific); 5. Outcomes: Primary - mental health outcomes at post-treatment (intervention vs comparator as mean, SD, n, per group and/or between group effect size; mental health outcomes considered will be validated symptom measures, diagnostic status, or trial author-defined response or remission), Secondary – Safety (adverse events: intervention vs control/comparator, active monitoring vs passive reporting), Other (potential) – dropout, adherence, engagement metrics maybe extracted; 6. Other: data privacy/ethics considerations, funding source, industry involvement, developer, conflicts of interest, trial author/developer description of AI-tool (verbatim).

We will conduct publication bias assessments if a meta-analysis is deemed feasible. This will include Eggers test and visual inspection of funnel plots, keeping in mind these typically require 10 or more studies for valid results. We will also assess any bias attributed to the potential missingness of individual included trial results.

### Certainty assessment

The certainty of evidence will be evaluated using the GRADE (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation) framework. This will include assessment of trial-level risk of bias, indirectness, imprecision, inconsistency and publication bias.

## OUTCOMES TO BE ANALYSED
### Main outcomes
Primary outcome domains are those associated with seven conditions commonly occurring in youth populations: anxiety, bipolar, depression, eating disorders, psychosis, substance use, and suicide/self-harm. These align with the seven categories captured within Orygen’s Youth Mental Health Evidence Map (ref). 

Outcomes can be: 1. symptom severity levels captured by validated patient reported or observer-rated symptom assessment scales, 2. diagnostic status captured by clinical interview, self-report or other means, and 3. trial author-defined response/remission.

Data format: mental health symptom outcomes will be extracted as mean, n, SD per group, between group effect size, and/or author narrative description where no statistics are reported. Diagnostic status and response remission will be extracted as number of events over number randomised per group. Where data format is not available, appropriate conversions may be made following Cochrane handbook recommendations (ref) to derive between group effect sizes. 

Time point: primary time is post-treatment, secondary follow-up timepoints will be analysed as available (short-term: </= 6 months; medium-term: >6 months to 12 months; longer term: > 12 months). 

### Additional outcomes

Safety outcomes recorded as adverse events in intervention vs control/comparator. Assessment will be made whether these are from active monitoring or passive event recording.

## PLANNED DATA SYNTHESIS

### Strategy for data synthesis
A full narrative synthesis will be conducted to evaluate the overall effectiveness of the interventions and the various types of mental health outcomes. 

Given heterogeneity between trials in terms of the modality of AI-tools, likely co-interventions, trial participant mental health status, and mental health outcomes, it is unlikely sufficiently similar studies will be located to meta-analyse between group effects. In the case where sufficiently similar studies are available, a pairwise random effects meta-analysis will be conducted. Otherwise, a descriptive synthesis of trials and their effect estimates on mental health outcome will be presented by AI tool modality (conversation agent, recommender system, adaptive intervention) and by mental health outcome domain. This descriptive approach may use techniques outlined in Synthesis Without Meta-Analysis (SWIM) guidance where vote counting by direction of effect may be implemented if effect sizes cannot be calculated. Where effect sizes are available or can be calculated, spread of effects may be synthesized.
